# Computational and Experimental Evaluation of Arylidene Hydrazilene Indenoimidazole Compounds Against Plasmodium berghei

## Overview
Malaria remains one of the world's leading infectious diseases, with the emergence of drug-resistant Plasmodium species posing a major challenge to current treatment strategies. Developing new antimalarial compounds and understanding how they interact with parasite targets are therefore critical areas of research.
This undergraduate research project investigated the antimalarial potential of Arylidene Hydrazinyl Indenoimidazole (AHI) compounds using both experimental (in vivo) and computational (in silico) approaches. By combining animal studies, molecular docking, gene expression analysis, and ADMET prediction, the project aimed to provide a comprehensive assessment of the therapeutic potential of AHIs against Plasmodium berghei.

## Research Question

## Objectives
The study sought to:
Evaluate the in vivo antimalarial activity of an AHI compound using a Plasmodium berghei mouse model.
Assess the effect of treatment on parasite clearance and host survival.
Investigate potential molecular targets through docking simulations.
Analyse publicly available transcriptomic data to support biological interpretation.
Predict the pharmacokinetic and toxicity properties (ADMET) of the test compound.

## Methodology
Experimental (In Vivo)
Mouse infection model using Plasmodium berghei
Administration of synthesized AHI compound
Monitoring of parasitaemia levels
Survival analysis

Computational (In Silico)
Gene expression analysis using GEO dataset GSE198587
Molecular docking using iGEMDOCK
Target protein: FANCJ-like Helicase
ADMET prediction

## Key Findings
The study demonstrated that:

Treatment with the AHI compound significantly reduced parasitaemia compared with untreated controls.
Mice receiving 25 mg/kg showed the greatest improvement in survival.
Molecular docking indicated strong binding interactions between the compound and FANCJ-like Helicase, a protein involved in parasite DNA replication and blood-stage development.
ADMET analysis suggested favourable drug-like properties for the compound.
